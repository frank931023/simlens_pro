Abstract.
Large Language Model (LLM) personas with explicit specifications of attributes, background, and behavioural tendencies are increasingly used to simulate human conversations for tasks such as user modeling, social reasoning, and behavioural analysis. Ensuring that persona-grounded simulations faithfully reflect human conversational behaviour is therefore critical. However, current evaluation practices largely rely on LLM-as-a-judge approaches, offering limited grounding in observable human behavior and producing opaque scalar scores. We address this gap by proposing Eval4Sim, an evaluation framework that measures how closely simulated conversations align with human conversational patterns across three complementary dimensions. Adherence captures how effectively persona backgrounds are implicitly encoded in generated utterances, assessed via dense retrieval with speaker-aware representations. Consistency evaluates whether a persona maintains a distinguishable identity across conversations, computed through authorship verification. Naturalness reflects whether conversations exhibit human-like flow rather than overly rigid or optimized structure, quantified through distributions derived from dialogue-focused Natural Language Inference. Unlike absolute or optimization-oriented metrics, Eval4Sim uses a human conversational corpus (i.e., PersonaChat) as a reference baseline and penalizes deviations in both directions, distinguishing insufficient persona encoding from over-optimized, unnatural behaviour. Although demonstrated on PersonaChat, the applicability of Eval4Sim extends to any conversational corpus containing speaker-level annotations.

1.Introduction
Refer to caption
Figure 1.General overview of the proposed Eval4Sim evaluation framework for persona simulation.
Persona grounding refers to the practice of conditioning a conversational agent’s behavior on an explicit representation of a user profile, commonly termed a persona (Jung and Beebe, 2016; Leary and Allen, 2011), that specifies attributes such as background, preferences, beliefs, and behavioural tendencies. In persona-grounded conversational AI, these specifications are not merely contextual hints, but normative constraints that are intended to consistently shape language generation across turns, topics, and interaction sessions (Tseng et al., 2024). A common implementation paradigm is to condition a LLM on a structured or semi-structured persona specification, either through prompting, fine-tuning, or retrieval-based mechanisms (Chen et al., 2023). Such LLM personas are now widely used to simulate human conversations for tasks such as user modelling, social reasoning, and behavioural analysis (Cho et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2025).

In this simulation setting, success cannot be reduced to surface-level fluency or contextual relevance. A simulator is expected to maintain a stable and distinguishable identity over time, expressing persona-specific cues, preferences, and conversational style across dialogues (Tseng et al., 2024). Deviations such as persona drift (Li et al., 2024), inconsistency (Li et al., 2025), or exaggerated trait expression (Bhandari et al., 2025) compromise both the realism and validity of the simulated behavior. As persona-grounded systems become more prevalent, establishing principled, interpretable, and behaviourally grounded evaluation methodologies becomes essential (Schaer et al., 2024). Without such methods, it remains unclear whether improvements in system performance correspond to more faithful persona realization or merely to optimization toward opaque, model-internal criteria. However, current practice often relies on “LLM-as-a-judge” paradigms, where an LLM assigns scores for attributes such as persona consistency or overall quality (Zheng et al., 2023; Desmond et al., 2025). While scalable, these approaches are sensitive to prompt design, can exhibit systematic scoring biases, and provide limited insights into how simulated behavior diverges from human conversation (Arabzadeh and Clarke, 2025; Dietz et al., 2025; Tseng et al., 2024).

To address this gap, we introduce Eval4Sim1, an evaluation framework that shifts the goal from generic “quality” scoring to measuring behavioural alignment with humans. Rather than comparing each generated turn to a single gold response, as in overlap-based protocols (Papineni et al., 2002; Lin, 2004; Fang et al., [n. d.]), Eval4Sim treats a human-to-human conversation corpus (e.g., PersonaChat (Zhang et al., 2018)) as a behavioural proxy and evaluates whether a simulator reproduces its conversational patterns. As illustrated in Figure 1, Eval4Sim operates over persona descriptions and conversations, producing three-dimensional alignment scores capturing complementary aspects of persona-grounded simulation.

The core challenge is balancing three complementary dimensions: adherence, consistency, and naturalness. In human conversation, speakers typically carry identity cues implicitly while maintaining grounded interaction (Ochs, 1990; Bucholtz and Hall, 2005). In simulation, however, these dimensions often conflict (Dietz et al., 2025). Consider a system simulating an introverted persona: To maximize adherence, a model might explicitly state “I am an introvert,” making the persona easy to infer but reducing naturalness, since humans rarely repeat trait declarations in casual dialogue (Chaudoir and Fisher, 2010). Conversely, a highly natural model may produce fluent turns while gradually attenuating persona cues, undermining consistency. A strong simulator should therefore match the trade-offs observed in human conversation, rather than independently maximizing a single score.

To capture this complex dynamic, Eval4Sim compares simulations to the human proxy using three complementary evaluations: (1) Adherence via Dense Retrieval: We quantify how attributable a conversation is to its persona by treating persona descriptions as queries and conversations as documents. Using ColBERT (Khattab and Zaharia, 2020a), we target retrieval alignment: personas should be recoverable from simulated dialogue with the same clarity (or ambiguity) as in human conversations. (2) Consistency via Authorship Verification: We measure identity stability across conversations by framing persona realization as same-author/different-author verification over stylometric signals. (3) Naturalness via NLI Distribution: We measure conversational flow by comparing NLI label distributions over turn-to-turn and persona-related pairs, capturing whether simulated dialogue is overly rigid (over-entailing) or incoherent (contradictory). The main contributions of this work are:

∙
 We propose Eval4Sim, an evaluation framework for persona-grounded conversation simulation that penalizes deviations from human behavior in both directions, enabling evaluation of under-expression versus over-optimization.

∙
 We implement three alignment dimensions: adherence, consistency, and naturalness, in a unified, corpus-agnostic pipeline.

∙
 We instantiate Eval4Sim on PersonaChat and evaluate ten simulation datasets. Our results show that (i) adherence is strongest for modern LLMs, (ii) consistency follows a different ordering, and (iii) all simulations shift toward higher entailment and lower neutrality than human dialogue, indicating overly “logically smooth” flow. Aggregating these dimensions, no simulator matches humans uniformly, while Qwen3 30B provides the best overall balance.

2.Related Work
Persona-grounded dialogue systems condition response generation on persona descriptions to produce consistent conversational behavior. The PersonaChat dataset (Zhang et al., 2018) pioneered this paradigm as an early representative benchmark, featuring crowdsourced dialogues where speakers adopt predefined personas consisting of at least five profile sentences. Methods for improving persona consistency include using NLI to encourage entailing utterances (Song et al., 2020), reinforcement learning for offline training of persona-consistent agents (Shea and Yu, 2023), and techniques that balance adherence to persona descriptions against conversational naturalness (Lin et al., 2025).

Evaluating persona-grounded dialogue is challenging because personas influence conversation in multiple ways. Traditional metrics such as BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002), Perplexity (Fang et al., [n. d.]), and ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004) correlate poorly with human judgments in open-domain dialogue (Liu et al., 2016), capturing surface overlap rather than persona consistency or conversational quality (Tseng et al., 2024). Specialized frameworks introduce persona-specific metrics (e.g., speaker probability and term salience (Uehara et al., 2021)), explicit consistency labels between profiles and responses (Welleck et al., 2019), and human evaluation protocols with multiple scales (See et al., 2019). However, these approaches often isolate single attributes and do not explicitly characterize trade-offs among conversational dimensions like adherence, consistency, and naturalness.

For adherence, information retrieval provides a practical assessment: if persona cues are expressed implicitly, a persona profile should reliably retrieve the corresponding conversation among distractors. Dense retrieval embeds queries and documents in a shared semantic space to match beyond lexical overlap. Sentence-BERT (Reimers and Gurevych, 2019) enabled efficient bi-encoder retrieval, while ColBERT (Khattab and Zaharia, 2020b) introduced late interaction for finer matching, with subsequent efficiency improvements such as ColBERTv2 (Santhanam et al., 2022). Adapting this to persona evaluation reverses the usual perspective: persona profiles serve as semantically rich queries and full conversations as candidate documents, so adherence corresponds to retrieval adherence: a conversation should be most strongly associated with its persona, at a level comparable to human dialogues.

For consistency, we draw on authorship verification, which asks whether two texts originate from the same author based on writing style. The PAN shared tasks at CLEF provide benchmark datasets and protocols for this setting (Kestemont et al., 2020; Bevendorff et al., 2023), and analogous methods can test whether personas exhibit distinguishable stylistic signatures across conversations. For naturalness, Natural Language Inference (NLI) offers a complementary signal (Dziri et al., 2019): NLI models label utterance pairs as entailment, contradiction, or neutral, and the resulting label distributions across turns compactly reflect dialogue flow (Welleck et al., 2019). By comparing NLI label distributions between human and generated dialogues, we can assess how closely a simulator matches human-like pragmatic flow without requiring reference responses at each turn (Welleck et al., 2019).

Recent work has begun to evaluate persona-grounded LLMs via large-scale benchmarks (Afzoon et al., 2024; Samuel et al., 2024). PersoBench (Afzoon et al., 2024) focuses on turn-level response generation (i.e., only evaluating one single response each time), scoring outputs with a mix of reference-based metrics and persona-consistency measures. PersonaGym (Samuel et al., 2024), in contrast, targets persona agents, introducing a dynamic evaluation setup where personas are tested across automatically generated environments and tasks. Its scoring is based on LLMs-as-a-judge: model outputs are graded with rubric-based judgments and aggregated into task-level persona scores. Our proposal differs from both lines of work by two main aspects:

3.Methodology
Eval4Sim evaluates persona-grounded conversation simulation along three complementary dimensions that capture distinct aspects of human-like dialogue: adherence to the persona descriptions, consistency of speaker identity across interactions, and naturalness of conversational flow. We use human-to-human conversations as reference and score simulators by their alignment to them, penalizing deviations in either direction. This allows us to distinguish between under-expression of persona information and exaggerated, unnatural persona signalling.

The framework operates on two inputs: (i) a reference corpus of human-to-human conversations that establishes behavioural baselines, and (ii) one or more simulation corpora produced by conversational simulators to evaluate. Both corpora contain multi-turn dialogues between two speakers, and each speaker is associated with a persona description. The framework is corpus-agnostic: any persona-grounded dataset with speaker-level persona annotations can serve as the reference, with the choice of reference determining what is considered “human-like” or target style of conversations for evaluation. 3.1.Adherence via Dense Retrieval
We define adherence as the ability of a simulator to implicitly express a persona’s traits and background such that the generated conversation is attributable to that persona (Zhang et al., 2018). To evaluate this ability, we model adherence as a retrieval task: persona descriptions as queries and generated conversations as documents. Intuitively, if a simulator consistently manifests persona cues in dialogue, the associated persona description should retrieve that conversation more reliably than unrelated conversations. For this reason, the task here is to rank conversations in which the personas participated higher than those in which they did not.

We define consistency as the ability of a simulator to maintain a stable and distinguishable speaker identity across conversations (Johnstone, 2000; Barlow, 2013): utterances produced under the same persona should exhibit coherent stylistic regularities, while remaining separable from other personas. While stronger consistency improves verification performance, excessive stylistic uniformity can produce repetitive dialogue that lacks the natural variation observed in human speakers. Eval4Sim therefore evaluates alignment to a human reference corpus: a simulator is preferred when its same vs. different separability matches the reference level, rather than exceeding it through repetitive or overly stylized generation.

4.Experiments and Results
This section describes the experimental instantiation of the Eval4Sim framework and reports results across the three evaluation dimensions. We first describe the experimental setup (reference corpus, adherence ranking configuration, and evaluated simulations), and then present adherence, consistency, and naturalness results for each simulation relative to the human baseline.

4.1.Experimental Setup
4.1.1.Reference Corpus
We instantiate the framework using PersonaChat (Zhang et al., 2018) as the reference corpus. PersonaChat consists of persona-grounded, human-to-human dialogues crowdsourced via Amazon Mechanical Turk, where each speaker is assigned a short profile of persona descriptions that conditions the conversation. In this work, we treat PersonaChat as the behavioural proxy that defines the target distribution for human-like simulation.

Two are well-known datasets: Synthetic-Persona-Chat (SPC) (Jandaghi et al., 2024), which reuses PersonaChat persona descriptions but replaces the conversations with synthetic dialogue, and Synthetic-Persona-Chat-New (SPC-New)(Jandaghi et al., 2023), which contains fully synthetic persona descriptions and conversations. Both datasets were created with the Generator–Critic framework (Jandaghi et al., 2024): an LLM Generator produces multiple candidate conversations for each persona pair, and an LLM-based Critic (implemented as a mixture of expert judges targeting general conversation quality) selects the best candidates to iteratively improve the generated dataset.

For each LLM, we generate five independent conversation sets (different random seeds). All results reported in the following sections are averaged across these five runs to account for generation variability. For the generated conversations by the LLMs, if a generation does not match the required format after five attempts, the samples are discarded.

4.2.Experiment 1: Adherence
We test whether simulated conversations encode persona information in a way that is attributable to the intended persona, and whether this encoding matches the level of implicitness observed in human dialogue. We instantiate adherence as a retrieval task: each conversation contains two speakers, and we evaluate the target persona (User 2) by retrieving the conversation they participated in using only target persona description as the query. As the candidate pool grows (more distractor conversations), retrieval becomes harder unless persona cues are expressed consistently across the target user’s utterances.

Our goal is not to maximize retrieval. Instead, we compare how retrieval degrades with difficulty and score each simulator by its alignment to the PersonaChat degradation pattern. Under-encoding typically manifests as consistently worse MRR than the human curve (persona cues are too weak or too generic). Over-encoding can manifest as systematically better MRR than humans (persona cues are unnaturally explicit or repeated), producing a curve that stays above the human baseline across pool sizes.

We test whether simulated personas maintain a stable, distinguishable identity across their utterances by measuring authorship verification behavior. The task is to decide whether two generated texts originate from the same persona (same-author) or from different personas (different-author). For each persona, we split its utterances into two parts to form same-author pairs, and we form different-author pairs by randomly pairing text samples from different personas.

We train a baseline method from the PAN workshop (Bevendorff et al., 2023), based on a character 4-gram TF–IDF verifier with a vocabulary size of 4000, as described in Section 3.2. 4.4.Experiment 3: Naturalness
We test whether simulated conversations exhibit human-like conversational flow comparable to human dialogue. Following Section 3.3, we apply a dialogue-focused NLI model to three types of text pairs: (i) consecutive turns to characterize local dialogue flow, (ii) persona–utterance pairs to detect conflicts with persona descriptions, and (iii) within-speaker historical pairs to detect self-contradictions. As in the other dimensions, our objective is alignment rather than metric maximization: human conversations are neither perfectly coherent nor strictly entailment-driven. Instead, they are dominated by neutral transitions that reflect pragmatic responses, topic drift, and elaboration, with relatively few contradictions.

4.4.1.Experimental Setting
For each dataset, we apply the DeBERTa-based Dialogue NLI model to classify the pairwise relations. We compute the metrics explained in Section 3.3, Coherence Score (CS) on consecutive turns, Persona Contradiction Rate (PCR) on persona–utterance pairs, and Self-Contradiction Rate (SCR) on within-speaker history, using a contradiction confidence threshold of 0.7 and a 5-turn window for self-contradiction checks. We aggregate these metrics into a Naturalness score and compute Similarity to PersonaChat using the bidirectional deviation penalty, so that deviations in either direction (under or over naturalness) from the baseline reduce similarity.

4.4.3.External validation.
To validate that PersonaChat’s NLI distribution reflects general human conversation, we additionally analyse 1,439 casual dialogues from three external datasets (Li et al., 2017; Porgali et al., 2023; Kaggle, 2020) with the same metrics. These conversations show a similarly neutral-dominant pattern (ER/NR/CR = 0.17/0.81/0.02), consistent with PersonaChat (0.16/0.80/0.05), confirming that human dialogue predominantly progresses through neutral transitions rather than strict entailment. In contrast, the simulated datasets (especially the Generator–Critic corpora) produce an overly “logically smooth” flow that reduces topic drift. Compared to Experiments 1 (Section 4.2) and 2 (Section 4.3), this finding is complementary: some corpora can appear highly coherent under NLI while still exhibiting weak adherence, indicating that logical coherence is not a proxy for persona-grounded realism.

4.5.Overall Evaluation: e4s
In the last experiment, we consolidate results across all three dimensions to assess e4s simulator alignment with human conversation. Table 6 reports normalized similarity scores for adherence (MRR similarity), consistency (authorship-verification similarity), and naturalness (NLI-based similarity), using PersonaChat as the reference baseline (1.000). We compute e4s as an overall score as the unweighted mean of the three dimensions, treating them as equally important facets of persona-grounded simulation quality.

Looking at Table 6, the main takeaway is that no evaluated simulator matches human behavior uniformly across dimensions. Instead, models occupy different points in the three dimensions. Qwen3 30B ranks first overall (0.950), combining strong adherence (0.974; rank 2) with the best naturalness alignment (0.960; rank 1), while remaining moderately close in consistency (rank 6). The next two overall performers are Gemma 3 12B (0.938) and Gemma 3 4B (0.935), both driven by very high adherence and strong naturalness, but penalized by weaker consistency alignment (0.877 and 0.901).

In contrast, the Generator–Critic datasets show the opposite pattern. SPC and SPC-New are among the closest to the human baseline in consistency (0.955 and 0.953; ranks 2 and 3), but they score lowest on adherence (ranks 10 and 9) and show substantial naturalness deviations (ranks 8 and 10), resulting in the lowest overall scores. This pattern suggests that the Generator–Critic pipeline encourages stable stylistic writing, but does not reliably express persona attributes in a retrievable manner and tends to produce overly “logically smooth” dialogue relative to human NLI profiles.

The mid-sized LLMs highlight that the trade-offs are not reducible to model scale alone. Qwen3 14B achieves the best consistency alignment overall (rank 1), yet it is among the weakest in naturalness alignment (rank 9). Meanwhile, Gemma 3 4B is the most balanced among the mid-sized systems (Overall 0.935; rank 3), illustrating that near-human alignment can emerge from a combination of moderate adherence, moderate consistency, and moderate naturalness rather than excellence on a single dimension.

5.Conclusions
We presented Eval4Sim, an evaluation framework for persona-grounded conversation simulation that measures alignment to human conversational behavior across three complementary dimensions: adherence (persona attribution via dense retrieval), consistency (identity stability via authorship verification), and naturalness (conversational flow via dialogue NLI patterns). In contrast to LLM-as-a-judge paradigms that return opaque scalar scores, Eval4Sim uses a human dialogue corpus as a behavioral baseline and penalizes deviations in either direction, separating under-expression of persona cues from over-optimized, unnatural behavior. Experiments over ten simulated corpora reveal clear trade-offs. LLM-generated conversations achieve the strongest adherence, while Generator–Critic datasets perform worse. Consistency exhibits a different ordering, with Qwen3 14B closest to the human baseline. For naturalness, all simulations shift toward higher entailment compared to human dialogue, indicating an overly “logically smooth” flow. When aggregating dimensions, no simulator matches humans uniformly; Qwen3 30B provides the best overall balance (0.950), combining strong adherence with the closest naturalness alignment. More generally, these results highlight why persona simulation should be evaluated multi-dimensionally: optimizing a single objective can move a simulator away from the human baseline on another axis. Eval4Sim provides a practical and interpretable foundation for guiding the development of persona simulators that better match how humans balance dialogue dynamics.
